# A Control-Theoretic Foundation for Agentic Systems 

Ali Eslami 1 and Jiangbo Yu 1

Abstract —This paper develops a control-theoretic framework for analyzing agentic systems embedded within feedback control loops. In such systems, an AI agent may adapt controller parameters, select among control strategies, invoke tools, recon-figure decision architectures, or modify control objectives during operation. We formalize these capabilities by interpreting agency as hierarchical decision authority over the control architecture. A unified dynamical representation is introduced that incorporates memory, learning, tool activation, interaction signals, and goal descriptors within a single closed-loop structure. Based on this representation, we define a five-level hierarchy of agency ranging from reactive rule-based control to the synthesis of control ob-jectives and controller architectures. The framework is presented in both nonlinear and linear settings, allowing agentic behaviors to be interpreted using standard control-theoretic constructs such as feedback gains, switching signals, parameter adaptation laws, and quadratic cost functions. The analysis shows that increasing agency introduces dynamical mechanisms including time-varying adaptation, endogenous switching, decision-induced delays, and structural reconfiguration of the control pipeline. This perspective provides a mathematical foundation for analyzing stability, safety, and performance of AI-enabled control systems. 

I. INTRODUCTION Modern AI systems are increasingly deployed within feed-back control loops, where they can adapt controller parame-ters, select among control strategies, invoke external compu-tational tools, and modify task objectives based on contextual information. This raises a fundamental question for the control community: when an AI system gains authority not only to compute control inputs but also to adapt parameters, select strategies, invoke tools, reconfigure architectures, or modify objectives during operation, what type of dynamical system must be analyzed? 

Traditional control theory provides well-established tools for analyzing fixed controllers [1], adaptive systems [2], [3], switched systems [4], and hybrid dynamical systems [5]. How-ever, agentic AI architectures may combine several of these mechanisms simultaneously within a single feedback loop. An AI agent may adapt controller gains online, switch among multiple control policies based on learned context, query external prediction or optimization modules, and revise control objectives in response to changing mission requirements or in-teraction signals. When such capabilities are embedded within a closed-loop system, the resulting dynamics can no longer be represented as a conventional fixed, adaptive, or switched controller. The decision-making process itself becomes part of the feedback loop, and the authority granted to the AI over different elements of the control architecture directly

In robotics, recent work demonstrates that language-model-based agents can reason about tasks, select skills, and orchestrate multiple controllers to execute complex behaviors [6]. In transportation systems, the emerg-ing concept of agentic vehicles describes vehicles equipped with AI agents capable of reasoning, planning, and adapting behavior within complex environments [7]. Broader analyses of human–machine transportation systems have emphasized that increasing levels of agency introduce not only new ca-pabilities but also operational, societal, and policy challenges [8]. Security analyses of agentic vehicles reveal that decision-making agents can introduce new classes of vulnerabilities that propagate across cognitive, software, and physical layers [9]. These examples illustrate that AI systems are transitioning from design-time assistants to runtime decision-makers across safety-critical cyber-physical systems, yet the control-theoretic implications of this transition remain largely unexplored. Existing literature on AI-enabled control has made substan-tial progress in integrating AI into specific control tasks. Inter-active design tools now allow LLM agents to assist engineers in controller tuning, such as PID parameter selection through natural language dialogue [10]. Multi-agent frameworks have been proposed where LLM agents collaborate to generate and refine candidate control strategies [11], and LLM-assisted toolboxes enable interactive design of adaptive controllers such as model reference adaptive control (MRAC) systems [12]. More recent work has explored deeper integrations: translating natural-language specifications into certified controllers using 

H∞ control and linear matrix inequalities [13], generating adaptive compensators that augment feedback controllers un-der uncertainty [14], incorporating LLM reasoning into model predictive control [15], and combining language models with safety-aware optimal control using control barrier functions [16]. Early demonstrations have shown that natural language instructions can be translated into closed-loop control actions in physical systems [17], and agent frameworks have been developed to assist with control-loop implementation through interfaces with digital twins and hardware [18]. While these contributions demonstrate the practical utility of AI-assisted control design, most remain application-specific and treat the AI module as a tuning assistant, supervisory optimizer, or natural-language interface layered on top of a classical controller. They do not provide a unified control-theoretic framework for understanding what agency means in feedback systems or how increasing AI decision author-ity affects the closed-loop dynamics that must be analyzed. In particular, when an AI system can simultaneously adapt parameters, switch among strategies, invoke tools, reconfigure 1

The key missing element is a mathematical representation of decision authority, the degree of control the AI has over different layers of the control architecture, and how this authority influences system stability, safety, and performance. These observations highlight a fundamental gap in the current literature. Although recent work has explored the integration of AI and control in specific applications, a unified control-theoretic framework for analyzing agentic AI systems embedded in feedback loops is still missing. To the best of our knowledge, this paper presents the first systematic attempt to formulate agentic AI systems within a control-theoretic framework. Motivated by this gap, this paper develops a control-theoretic framework for agentic AI systems by interpreting agency as hierarchical decision authority over the control architecture. Rather than treating memory, learning, tool use, interaction, and goal formulation as disconnected capabilities, we embed them into a unified dynamical representation. In this view, increasing agency corresponds to granting the AI authority over progressively deeper elements of the control stack: controller parameters, controller families, tool activa-tion, workflow composition, and goal descriptors. This per-spective enables abstract agentic capabilities to be interpreted using familiar control-theoretic constructs such as state-space models, feedback gains, switching signals, parameter adapta-tion laws, and optimization objectives. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:  

> •

We propose a unified nonlinear dynamical formulation of agentic AI control systems that explicitly incorporates memory, learning, tool invocation, interaction signals, and goal representations within a single closed-loop architec-ture.  

> •

We introduce a five-level hierarchy of agency that formal-izes increasing decision authority over the control stack, ranging from reactive rule-based control to generative synthesis of control objectives and workflows under gov-ernance constraints.  

> •

We specialize the framework to linear dynamical systems and provide concrete interpretations of agency in terms of feedback gains, admissible objectives, controller families, and modular control architectures.  

> •

We analyze how increasing levels of agency intro-duce mechanisms associated with time-varying systems, switched systems, hybrid dynamics, and objective-driven policy variation, providing insight into the stability im-plications of agentic decision-making. By casting agentic AI systems in control-theoretic terms, this work provides a mathematical foundation for analyzing how AI-driven decision authority affects stability, safety, and performance in feedback systems. The proposed framework bridges the gap between emerging agentic AI architectures and rigorous dynamical systems analysis, enabling systematic study of AI-enabled control systems across domains including robotics, mobility systems, and cyber-physical infrastructures.

F. Interpretation 

The formulation above unifies the main agentic functions into a single dynamical architecture. The controller is no longer viewed as a static policy acting only on measurements. Instead, it is a structured decision system whose behavior depends on internal memory, learning variables, external tool outputs, interaction signals, and goal descriptors. Increasing agency corresponds to increasing authority over these ele-ments. This viewpoint is especially useful because it avoids a fragmented description of agentic AI in terms of disconnected capabilities. Instead, all capabilities appear as coordinated components of a single closed-loop decision architecture. In the proposed framework, higher agency does not sim-ply mean improved task performance or more sophisticated computation. Rather, it means that the AI is granted authority over progressively deeper layers of the control architecture. At low agency, the AI reacts within a fixed policy structure. At higher levels, it may adapt parameters, select strategies, compose tools, and eventually formulate new goals under external constraints. Memory at Level 1: Memory is minimal and operational only. The controller may retain a mode flag, timer, or small finite-state variable, but there is no semantic, persistent, or learned memory. Any such state is preprogrammed and serves only to implement reactive logic. 

Interaction and Engagement at Level 1: Interaction is passive. The AI may receive commands or signals from a human operator, but only through a fixed interface and with-out dialogue, negotiation, or contextual interpretation beyond preset rules. In a multi-agent extension, this corresponds to hard-coded message handling rather than adaptive interaction. Therefore, Level 4 is best interpreted as structural agency ,in which the AI can configure and orchestrate the internal decision architecture.

In a multi-agent setting, this naturally corresponds to distributed goal formation and emergent coordination under system-level rules. Hence, Level 5 represents generative agency , where the AI is no longer restricted to selecting among predefined alter-natives but may synthesize elements of the decision problem itself.

The hierarchy above is a hierarchy of decision authority over the unified control architecture. The distinction among the levels can be summarized as follows:  

> •

Level 1: the AI reacts within fixed designer-specified rules;  

> •

Level 2: the AI adapts internal parameters and memory within a fixed structure;  

> •

Level 3: the AI selects among predefined goals, tools, and controller families;  

> •

Level 4: the AI reconfigures and composes the decision architecture;

Level 5: the AI generates new goals and workflows under governance constraints. 6IV. L INEAR AGENTIC CONTROL FORMULATION 

To complement the general nonlinear formulation, we now specialize the proposed framework to linear dynamical sys-tems. The linear case is useful for two reasons. First, it provides a more explicit interpretation of agency in terms of concrete control objects such as feedback gains, quadratic objectives, and controller architectures. Second, it offers a mathematically transparent setting in which the effect of increasing agency can later be related to switching, adaptation, modular interconnection, and objective variation. At the same time, the linear setting has important limitations, especially at high levels of agency, and these limitations are discussed at the end of this section.

V. A GENCY LEVELS FOR LINEAR SYSTEMS 

In the linear setting, the proposed hierarchy can be ex-pressed particularly clearly. Rather than forcing every capa-bility into a separate linear model at every level, the focus is placed on the overall authority granted to the AI over the control architecture. Each level below is therefore tied to the most natural linear object associated with that degree of agency: a fixed feedback law, adaptive gains, switching among predefined objectives, modular composition of linear subsys-tems, or synthesis of admissible objectives and architectures. 

A. Level 1: Reactive Linear Agency 

At Level 1, the AI has minimal authority. The controller is essentially a fixed linear law, or a fixed collection of linear laws selected by a designer-specified rule. A representative form is 

u(t) = Kσr (t)χ(t), σr (t) = R y(t), r (t), (49) where R(·) is a predefined rule. In the simplest case, (49) reduces to 

u(t) = Kχ (t). (50) 

Agency Interpretation: At this level, the AI does not mean-ingfully design or adapt the controller. It merely executes fixed reactions within a designer-prescribed linear structure. 

Goal Formulation: Goal formulation is fixed offline: 

Qζ(t) = ¯Q, Rζ(t) = ¯R. (51) The AI neither selects nor adapts the objective. 

Tool Use: Tool use is absent or fixed as part of the pipeline. Any estimator, filter, or monitor is predefined and not under agentic control. 

Learning: There is no online learning: 

θ(t) ≡ ¯θ. (52) 

Memory and Interaction: Memory is minimal and op-erational only, for example a simple mode flag or timer. Interaction is passive and handled through fixed logic without strategic interpretation. Accordingly, Level 1 corresponds to reactive linear automa-tion with negligible agentic authority .

Tool Use: Tool use becomes conditional but still bounded. The AI may activate or deactivate approved modules, such as a disturbance observer, anomaly monitor, or prediction block, but cannot strategically redesign workflows. 

Learning: Learning is central at this level. The AI may adapt gain coefficients, thresholds, weighting parameters, or internal model parameters. 

Memory and Interaction: Memory is persistent and func-tional, as modeled by (37). Interaction is adaptive but not strategic; the AI may remember recent commands or contex-tual information, but it does not yet negotiate or choose among high-level alternatives. Therefore, Level 2 corresponds to adaptive linear agency within a fixed control structure .

At Level 3, the AI may select among predefined controllers or predefined quadratic objectives according to context. A representative form is

Goal Formulation: Goal formulation takes the form of

Tool Use: Tool use becomes strategic. The AI may select which approved linear module or auxiliary estimator to rely on in different contexts.

Interaction becomes strategic in the sense that hu-man or multi-agent signals may influence which admissible mode is chosen. Hence, Level 3 corresponds to strategic linear agency through bounded selection among predefined gains, objectives, and modules .

D. Level 4: Structural Linear Agency 

At Level 4, the AI is allowed to configure the linear control architecture itself through composition of linear modules. Instead of merely selecting one gain from a fixed set, the AI may configure a structured controller composed of several interconnected linear blocks. A representative form is

Agency Interpretation: The AI now has authority not only over parameter values or mode selection, but also over the structured interconnection of linear decision modules. 

Goal Formulation: Goal formulation remains constrained, but the AI may coordinate structured objectives with the architecture. For example, safety may be enforced through one module while performance optimization is handled by another. The AI can therefore organize layered objective structures within an approved design space. 

Tool Use: Tool use reaches the level of composition and orchestration. The AI may combine multiple linear modules sequentially or in parallel, choose routing logic among them, and reconfigure the information-processing pipeline. 

Learning: Learning may affect not only gains but also the structural logic by which modules are interconnected or switched. 

Memory and Interaction: Memory becomes richer and architecture-aware, supporting escalation, fallback, or multi-stage decision logic. Interaction becomes collaborative in the sense that supervisory inputs or peer signals may influence architecture selection. Accordingly, Level 4 corresponds to structural linear agency through composition and reconfiguration of linear control modules .

Goal Formulation: Goal formulation becomes generative in a restricted linear sense: the AI may synthesize new admissible (Q, R ) pairs rather than merely selecting one from a predefined library. 

Tool Use: Tool use may also become generative in the sense of synthesizing new admissible workflows or new modular interconnections within a constrained linear architecture space. 

Learning: Learning becomes meta-level, because the AI may adapt not only gains and switching rules, but also the mechanism by which admissible objectives and architectures are synthesized. 

Memory and Interaction: Memory becomes long-horizon and mission-level, while interaction may become proactive or negotiation-oriented, still subject to governance constraints. Therefore, Level 5 corresponds to generative linear agency under a governed admissible design space .

F. Discussion and Limitations of the Linear Setting 

The linear hierarchy above is useful because it provides a precise and interpretable surrogate for agency in terms of familiar control objects such as gain matrices, quadratic objectives, and modular interconnections. In particular:  

> •

Level 1 is naturally represented by fixed linear feedback;  

> •

Level 2 is naturally represented by adaptive gains or bounded parameter tuning;  

> •

Level 3 is naturally represented by switching among predefined controllers or quadratic objectives;  

> •

Level 4 can be represented through composition and reconfiguration of linear modules;  

> •

Level 5 can be represented only in a restricted sense through governed synthesis of admissible linear objec-tives and architectures. However, the linear formulation also has clear limitations. Levels 1–3 are represented naturally and with relatively little ambiguity. Level 4 can still be captured meaningfully when interpreted as structured interconnection or supervisory com-position of linear subsystems. In contrast, Level 5 is only partially represented in a linear framework. Truly open-ended goal generation, symbolic planning, combinatorial workflow synthesis, or unconstrained architecture invention are not nat-urally linear phenomena. Hence, the linear model should be interpreted as a restricted but useful approximation of high-agency behavior, rather than a full description of all possible forms of agentic AI. This limitation is not a weakness of the framework; rather, it clarifies the scope of the linear analysis. The linear sec-tion provides a mathematically transparent specialization of the general theory, while the broader nonlinear formulation remains necessary for capturing the full richness of structural and generative agency. 

G. Summary of Linear Agency Levels 

The agency hierarchy for linear systems may therefore be summarized as follows:  

> •

Level 1: fixed linear feedback or rule-based switching among fixed gains;  

> •

Level 2: adaptive gains or bounded tuning within a fixed structure;  

> •

Level 3: switching among predefined controller structures or predefined objectives;  

> •

Level 4: structured composition and reconfiguration of linear control modules;  

> •

Level 5: governed synthesis of admissible linear objec-tives and controller architectures. This linear hierarchy provides a concrete bridge between the abstract notion of agency and standard control-theoretic constructs. It also sets the stage for a later stability discussion, in which the effect of adaptation, switching, modular reconfig-uration, and objective synthesis on the closed-loop dynamics can be analyzed explicitly. VI. S TABILITY IMPLICATIONS OF INCREASING AGENCY 

In the proposed architecture, the level of agency determines how much authority the AI has over the control system. As this authority increases, the controller can modify parameters, select strategies, invoke tools, or change the control architec-ture. These capabilities introduce new dynamical mechanisms in the closed-loop system. The goal of this section is not to claim that agentic control systems are necessarily unstable. Instead, the purpose is to highlight how increasing agency introduces mechanisms that may affect stability. These mechanisms correspond to well-known classes of dynamical systems such as time-varying systems, switched systems, delay systems, and hybrid systems. 

A. Adaptation-Induced Time Variation (Level 2) 

At Level 2, the AI is allowed to adapt controller parameters online. The control law may be written as 

u(t) = K(θ(t)) χ(t), (64) where θ(t) represents parameters updated through learning or adaptation. 10 As a result, the closed-loop system becomes time-varying: 

˙x(t) = Acl (θ(t)) x(t). (65) Even if the matrix Acl (θ) is stable for each fixed value of θ,stability of the overall system is not automatically guaranteed when the parameters vary with time. In classical adaptive control, stability is typically ensured by restricting the update law or by enforcing a separation between the adaptation time scale and the plant dynamics. If parameter updates occur too rapidly, the resulting time variation may degrade stability or performance. Thus, adaptive agency introduces a time-varying control law whose stability properties depend on the dynamics of the parameter updates. 

B. Endogenous Switching (Level 3) 

At Level 3, the AI can choose among multiple control strategies, goals, or tools. In this case the control law may take the form 

u(t) = Kσ(t)χ(t), (66) where σ(t) denotes the selected control mode. A key difference from classical switched systems is that the switching signal is generated internally by the AI decision process, 

σ(t) = Σ( χ(t)) , (67) where the decision depends on measurements, memory states, tool outputs, or interaction signals. Interaction signals may also contain semantic ambiguity due to language-based interpretation mechanisms. Such ambiguity affects the information available to the decision process and may influence goal selection, controller switching, or tool invocation. This means that even if the plant and nominal controller are not switched systems, the introduction of agentic decision-making can transform the closed-loop system into a switched system. 

C. Decision-Induced Delays (Level 3–5) 

Higher levels of agency require the AI to perform additional reasoning steps, such as querying tools, evaluating strategies, or communicating with other agents. These processes may introduce delays in the feedback loop. For example, the control input may depend on delayed information: 

u(t) = Kx (t − τ (t)) . (68) Similarly, switching decisions may depend on delayed tool outputs or communication signals. Delays are well known to affect stability in control systems. When delays become large or highly variable, they can reduce stability margins and lead to oscillatory behavior. In agentic control architectures, these delays arise naturally from the decision-making process and therefore must be considered when analyzing closed-loop stability. 

D. Structural Changes in the Control Architecture (Level 4) 

At Level 4, the AI can modify how the controller itself is organized. Instead of selecting only parameter values or control modes, the AI may configure different control ar-chitectures. For example, the system may switch between different pipelines such as direct feedback control, observer-based control, or planner-based control with a safety filter. This can be represented abstractly as 

u = Π( χ(t); C(t)) , (69) where C(t) denotes the selected architecture. When the architecture changes, the structure of the closed-loop system may also change. Different architectures may introduce different internal states or processing steps. As a result, the closed-loop dynamics may change across modes, producing a hybrid system whose behavior depends on the selected architecture. 

E. Objective-Induced Policy Variation (Level 5) 

At the highest level of agency, the AI may modify or generate new control objectives during operation. Because the control policy typically depends on the chosen objective, changes in the objective lead to changes in the synthesized controller. For example, in an optimal control setting the control law may be written as 

u(t) = K(Q(t), R (t)) x(t), (70) where the matrices Q(t) and R(t) are determined by the goal descriptor selected or generated by the AI. Note that the control gain can be dependent not just on R(t) and Q(t), but on other parameters and cost function weights as well. Substituting into the plant dynamics yields 

˙x(t) =  A − BK (Q(t), R (t)) x(t). (71) Thus, changes in the objective induce changes in the closed-loop dynamics through the synthesized controller. If the ob-jective varies over time, the resulting system may behave as a time-varying or switched system whose stability depends on how frequently and how drastically the objectives are modified. VII. D ISCUSSION 

This paper proposes a control-theoretic view of agency in AI-based control systems. In the proposed framework, increasing agency means that the AI gains authority over different parts of the control stack. At low levels, the AI mainly computes the control input. At higher levels, it may adapt parameters, choose among control strategies, invoke tools, or modify the controller structure. This perspective helps interpret agentic AI using concepts that are familiar in control theory. An important observation is that increasing agency changes the type of dynamical system that must be analyzed. When the controller can adapt parameters, the closed loop becomes time-varying. When it can select among strategies, the system may behave as a switched system. Tool use and reasoning steps 11 may introduce delays in the loop. Finally, if the controller can modify its architecture, the resulting system becomes hybrid. These mechanisms are well known in control theory, but they appear naturally in agentic systems because the decision process itself becomes part of the feedback loop. This viewpoint also has implications for control design and verification. If the AI has authority over parameters, switching decisions, or controller structures, then mechanisms such as limits on adaptation rates, dwell-time constraints for switching, and delay-aware analysis may become important tools for regulating agentic controllers. In this sense, agency can be interpreted as a form of decision authority that must be managed to preserve desired closed-loop properties. Interaction signals also play an important role in agentic ar-chitectures. In many applications, the interaction variable r(t)

may represent commands or feedback from a human operator. When interaction is mediated through language-based inter-faces, semantic ambiguity may arise in the interpretation of the received message. In this paper, such ambiguity is treated only at a conceptual level, as a possible source of imperfect information for the decision process. A deeper analysis of interaction-driven uncertainty and semantic ambiguity is left for future work. Finally, several limitations should be noted. The present work focuses on modeling and conceptual analysis rather than on deriving complete stability guarantees for all agency levels. The linear formulation used in the paper provides a clear interpretation of agency, but it cannot fully represent open-ended reasoning or goal generation. In addition, the analysis mainly considers single-agent decision architectures. Multi-agent settings may introduce additional challenges related to communication, coordination, and interaction uncertainty. Despite these limitations, the proposed framework provides a useful starting point for studying agentic AI systems from a control perspective. It shows how concepts such as memory, tool use, interaction, and goal selection can be interpreted within a dynamical systems framework, and it highlights how increasing agency affects the structure and analysis of the closed-loop system. This paper proposed a control-theoretic framework for mod-eling agentic AI systems as hierarchical decision architectures embedded within feedback control loops. In the proposed formulation, agency is interpreted as decision authority over different elements of the control architecture, including con-troller parameters, goal selection, tool invocation, and con-troller structure. The analysis highlights that increasing agency does not inherently imply instability, but it introduces additional dynamical mechanisms that must be considered in stability analysis. In particular, adaptive agency leads to time-varying systems, strategic selection introduces switching behavior, rea-soning and tool usage may introduce delays, and architecture reconfiguration produces hybrid dynamics. This perspective helps bridge the conceptual gap between emerging agentic AI capabilities and established dynamical systems analysis in control theory. Several directions remain for future research. First, the present work focuses primarily on modeling and conceptual analysis; a natural next step is to derive formal stability guaran-tees for agentic control architectures, including conditions un-der which adaptation, switching, architectural reconfiguration, or objective updates preserve closed-loop stability and perfor-mance. Second, the framework should be extended to multi-agent settings, where multiple agentic controllers interact with each other and with shared environments. Such systems may exhibit coordination dynamics, communication delays, and emergent behaviors that require new analysis tools from multi-agent systems and distributed control. Third, practical design guidelines are needed for incorporating agentic AI into control systems, particularly in safety-critical applications. Examples include limiting adaptation rates, imposing dwell-time con-straints on controller switching, bounding delays introduced by reasoning processes, and enforcing constraints on objective or architecture synthesis. Additional directions include studying robustness to model uncertainty in agentic controllers, analyz-ing the effects of semantic ambiguity in interaction signals, 14 and developing verification and certification methods for AI-driven control architectures.
